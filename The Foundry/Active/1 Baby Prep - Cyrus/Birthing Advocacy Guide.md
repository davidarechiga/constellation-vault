# Birthing Advocacy Guide

> **For David**: Your role as birth partner and advocate for Camille during labor and delivery
> **Related**: [[finalize-birth-plan]] • [[Childbirth Intensive Alta Bates]] • [[Mindfulness Birthing Class]]

---

## 🎯 Your Core Role as Birth Partner

**You are Camille's advocate, protector, and support person.** Your job is to:
1. **Protect her space** - Keep the environment calm and aligned with her preferences
2. **Communicate for her** - Be the voice when she's focused on labor
3. **Provide physical support** - Help with positions, comfort measures, hydration
4. **Offer emotional reassurance** - Remind her of her strength and progress
5. **Make decisions together** - Use BRAIN framework when choices arise

**Remember**: Labor is unpredictable. Stay flexible, trust Camille's instincts, and work as a team with Verenice (doula) and the medical staff.

---

## 🧠 The BRAIN Framework - Your Decision-Making Tool

**Use BRAIN for EVERY medical decision or intervention suggested during labor.**

When medical staff suggests an intervention or procedure, ask these questions:

### **B - Benefits**
"What are the potential benefits of this intervention?"
- How will this help Camille or the baby?
- What improvement are we expecting to see?

### **R - Risks**
"What are the risks or downsides?"
- What could go wrong?
- What are the side effects or complications?
- How common are these risks?

### **A - Alternatives**
"What other options do we have?"
- Are there other approaches we could try first?
- What about less invasive options?
- Can we modify the suggested intervention?

### **I - Intuition**
"What does your gut tell you? What feels right?"
- Check in with Camille: How does she feel about this?
- Does this align with your birth plan and values?
- Trust her instincts about her body

### **N - Nothing / Not Now**
"What happens if we wait or do nothing?"
- Is this urgent, or do we have time to think?
- Can we try nothing for 30 minutes and reassess?
- What are we monitoring if we choose to wait?

---

### How to Use BRAIN in the Moment

**When staff suggests an intervention:**

1. **Ask for time**: *"Can you give us a few minutes to discuss this?"*
2. **Step aside** with Camille (if possible) and Verenice
3. **Walk through BRAIN together**:
   - What are the benefits? (medical reason for this)
   - What are the risks? (what could go wrong)
   - What are alternatives? (other options)
   - What does intuition say? (how does Camille feel)
   - What if we wait? (is this urgent or can we reassess)
4. **Make the decision as a team** - you + Camille + Verenice
5. **Communicate clearly**: *"We've decided to [accept/decline/wait]. Here's why..."*

**Example in action:**

**Nurse**: "We'd like to start Pitocin to speed up labor."

**You**: "Can you give us a few minutes to discuss this?"

**BRAIN Discussion (with Camille & Verenice):**
- **B**: Pitocin strengthens contractions, may speed up dilation
- **R**: Stronger/more painful contractions, may increase need for epidural, potential fetal distress
- **A**: Continue natural labor, try position changes, wait longer before intervening
- **I**: Camille feels like her body is progressing fine, wants to avoid Pitocin
- **N**: If we wait 1-2 hours, labor may progress naturally; we can reassess then

**Decision**: "We appreciate the suggestion, but we'd like to give Camille's body more time. Let's reassess in 1-2 hours and see how labor is progressing naturally."

---

### When BRAIN Indicates "Yes, Proceed"

**Trust medical staff when:**
- Benefits clearly outweigh risks
- There's a medical emergency (baby's heart rate dropping, hemorrhage, etc.)
- Alternatives aren't viable or safe
- Camille's intuition says "yes, this feels right"
- Waiting poses greater risk than acting

**Example**: Baby's heart rate is dropping consistently → C-section recommended → BRAIN framework supports proceeding urgently

---

### When BRAIN Indicates "No" or "Not Now"

**Push back when:**
- No clear medical indication ("this is just standard protocol")
- Risks outweigh benefits for your specific situation
- Viable alternatives exist that you'd prefer to try first
- Camille's intuition says "this doesn't feel right"
- Waiting/observing is a safe option

**Example**: Labor is progressing normally, staff suggests breaking water to "speed things up" → BRAIN framework supports declining and continuing natural progression

---

### BRAIN Quick Reference Card

**Memorize these 5 questions:**

1. **Benefits** - "How will this help?"
2. **Risks** - "What could go wrong?"
3. **Alternatives** - "What else can we try?"
4. **Intuition** - "What does Camille feel is right?"
5. **Nothing** - "What if we wait?"

**Keep this in your hospital bag** - write it on a notecard or save in phone notes for quick reference.

## ⚠️ CRITICAL: Advocating Against Medical Racism & Implicit Bias

**This section is essential reading. Camille's life may depend on your advocacy.**

### The Reality: Black Maternal Health Crisis

**The facts:**
- Black women in the U.S. are **3-4 times more likely to die** from pregnancy-related causes than white women
- Black women are **2-3 times more likely** to experience severe maternal morbidity (life-threatening complications)
- **This disparity exists across ALL income and education levels** - even highly educated, affluent Black women face higher mortality rates
- These disparities are **primarily driven by systemic racism, implicit bias, and dismissal of Black women's concerns** by medical providers

**What this means for you:**
You are not just Camille's birth partner - **you are her first line of defense against medical racism**. Your advocacy could literally save her life.

---

### Understanding Implicit Bias in Healthcare

**Implicit bias** = unconscious beliefs that affect how medical providers treat Black patients

**How it shows up during childbirth:**
- **Pain dismissal**: Black women's pain is systematically underestimated and undertreated
  - Providers may assume Black women have "higher pain tolerance" (a racist myth)
  - Complaints of severe pain may be dismissed as "overreacting" or "dramatic"
  - Requests for pain relief may be delayed or denied

- **Concern dismissal**: Black women's medical concerns are often not taken seriously
  - Symptoms like severe headaches, vision changes, chest pain may be minimized
  - Complaints of "something's wrong" may be attributed to anxiety or fear
  - Warning signs of preeclampsia, hemorrhage, or other complications may be ignored

- **Communication barriers**: Staff may talk *around* Camille instead of *to* her
  - Providers may direct questions/explanations to you (as a man) instead of her
  - Medical jargon used without explanation
  - Rushed explanations or impatience with questions

- **Assumptions about compliance**: Providers may assume Black patients won't follow instructions
  - Birth plan preferences may be dismissed as "unrealistic"
  - Choices may be questioned more than they would be for white patients

**The viral video reality**: There have been multiple viral videos in 2025 showing Black women in labor being ignored, dismissed, or denied care while in severe distress. These are not isolated incidents - they reflect systemic patterns.

### Your Advocacy Toolkit: Specific Strategies

#### 1. **Be Hypervigilant About Pain & Symptoms**

**If Camille says she's in severe pain:**
- ✅ "My wife is in severe pain. She needs pain management now."
- ✅ "What specific medical reason is there for delaying pain relief?"
- ✅ "This is not acceptable. We need a doctor/charge nurse now."

**If she reports concerning symptoms:**
- ✅ Document everything (time, symptom, who she told, response)
- ✅ "My wife is reporting [symptom]. This could indicate [preeclampsia/hemorrhage/etc]. What are you doing to assess this?"
- ✅ If dismissed: "I need you to document in her chart that she reported this symptom and your clinical reasoning for not investigating further."

**Warning signs that should NEVER be dismissed:**
- Severe headache that won't go away
- Vision changes (blurry, spots, flashing lights)
- Severe abdominal pain (upper right side especially)
- Chest pain or difficulty breathing
- Excessive bleeding (soaking through pad in <1 hour)
- Sudden swelling in face, hands, feet
- Baby's movement decreasing significantly

#### 2. **Demand to Be Heard**

**If staff is dismissing Camille's concerns:**
- ✅ "My wife knows her body. Something is wrong. We need a full evaluation now."
- ✅ "I'm not comfortable with this explanation. I want a second opinion."
- ✅ "Please document in her chart exactly what she said and your response."

**If they say "This is normal in labor":**
- ✅ "I understand labor is intense, but this seems different. Walk me through the specific clinical signs that tell you this is normal."
- ✅ "What would convince you this ISN'T normal? What are we watching for?"

**If you feel she's being treated differently:**
- ✅ "I need to speak with the charge nurse and a patient advocate immediately."
- ✅ "Would you be responding this way if she were white? Because it feels like we're not being heard."

#### 3. **Escalate When Necessary**

**Chain of escalation** (use when care is inadequate):
1. **Nurse** → Ask for charge nurse
2. **Charge Nurse** → Ask for attending OB/physician
3. **Attending OB** → Ask for patient advocate + hospital supervisor
4. **Hospital Administration** → File formal complaint, request different care team

**How to escalate:**
- ✅ "I need to speak with your supervisor/the charge nurse/the attending physician right now."
- ✅ "I'm requesting a patient advocate. How do I reach them?"
- ✅ "This is unacceptable care. I want this conversation documented and I want to speak with hospital administration."

**Don't worry about being "difficult"** - Camille's life is more important than being polite or likable.

#### 4. **Use Your Privilege**

**As Camille's partner, you may receive different treatment from staff.** Use this to her advantage:

- Ask the same questions Camille asks - providers may take them more seriously coming from you
- If staff directs answers to you instead of her, redirect: "Please speak to my wife - she's the patient and decision-maker."
- If you notice staff is more responsive to you, use that access to advocate: "My wife just told you she's in severe pain. What are we doing about that?"

**This is uncomfortable but necessary.** You may have to leverage the fact that providers respond differently to men or non-Black people. It's not fair, but use it.

#### 5. **Document Everything**

**Keep a written log** (phone notes work fine):
- Time + symptom Camille reports
- Who she told (nurse name, doctor name)
- Their response or action
- Time of follow-up (if any)

**Why this matters:**
- Creates accountability
- Provides evidence if you need to escalate
- Useful if filing complaint or legal action later

**How to document in the moment:**
- "I'm writing down what you just said and the time, so we have a record."
- Take photos of medical charts/monitors if something concerning is happening (ask permission if possible, but prioritize safety)

#### 6. **Leverage Verenice (Your Doula)**

**Verenice is experienced and knows the system.** Use her:
- "Verenice, does this seem right to you?"
- "Verenice, can you help us communicate with the medical team?"
- Ask her to escalate if you're being ignored

**Research shows**: The presence of a doula or support person reduces obstetric racism by nearly 50%. Verenice's presence matters.

#### 7. **Know the Specific Risks for Black Mothers**

**Conditions that disproportionately affect Black women:**
- **Preeclampsia/Eclampsia** (high blood pressure, seizures)
  - Symptoms: Severe headache, vision changes, upper abdominal pain, swelling
  - Can progress rapidly and become life-threatening

- **Postpartum Hemorrhage** (excessive bleeding after birth)
  - Normal blood loss: ~500ml vaginal, ~1000ml C-section
  - Concern: Soaking through pad in <1 hour, large clots, dizziness, rapid heartbeat

- **Cardiovascular Complications** (heart issues)
  - Symptoms: Chest pain, shortness of breath, rapid heartbeat, extreme fatigue

**If Camille has ANY of these symptoms, advocate aggressively.** Don't let staff minimize them.

### What to Do If You're Being Ignored or Stonewalled

**When staff is not responsive:**

1. **Ask for names and credentials**: "What's your name and title?" (Write it down)

2. **Request patient advocate**: "I need to speak with a patient advocate right now. How do I reach them?"

3. **Escalate to administration**: "I need the charge nurse and the hospital supervisor here immediately."

4. **Call the OB directly**: If your OB/midwife isn't there, call their after-hours line and explain the situation

5. **Document and report**: "I'm documenting this interaction, including your name, the time, and your refusal to provide appropriate care."

6. **As a last resort - call 911**: If Camille is in distress and hospital staff is not responding, call 911 from inside the hospital. This forces a rapid response and creates a paper trail.

**Do not be afraid to be "difficult," "demanding," or "aggressive."** Camille's life is more important than anyone's comfort.

### After Birth: Postpartum Vigilance

**The risk doesn't end at delivery.** Many Black maternal deaths occur in the days/weeks after birth.

**Warning signs in the first 6 weeks postpartum:**
- Severe headache or vision changes
- High blood pressure (≥140/90)
- Excessive bleeding (soaking pad in <1 hour)
- Severe abdominal or chest pain
- Difficulty breathing
- Swelling in legs with pain/redness
- Fever over 100.4°F
- Signs of infection at incision site (C-section)

**If Camille reports ANY of these:**
- Do not wait for regular follow-up appointment
- Call OB immediately, go to ER if urgent
- Advocate just as hard as you did during labor
- "My wife just gave birth and is having [symptoms]. Black women have higher rates of postpartum complications. She needs to be seen now."

## 🛡️ Advocacy Essentials

### The BRAIN Framework (for ALL decisions)

When medical staff suggests an intervention or procedure, use **BRAIN** to make informed decisions:

- **B - Benefits**: What are the potential benefits of this intervention?
- **R - Risks**: What are the risks or downsides?
- **A - Alternatives**: What other options do we have?
- **I - Intuition**: What does your gut tell you? What feels right?
- **N - Nothing/Not Now**: What happens if we wait or do nothing?

**How to use it**:
- Ask: *"Can you give us a few minutes to discuss this?"*
- Step aside with Camille (if possible) and talk through BRAIN together
- Make the decision as a team - you + Camille + Verenice

### Questions to Ask Medical Staff

**When they suggest an intervention:**
- "What's happening right now that makes this necessary?"
- "Is this urgent, or do we have time to think about it?"
- "What are the benefits and risks of doing this?"
- "What are our other options?"
- "What happens if we wait 30 minutes / 1 hour?"

**If they say "This is standard protocol":**
- "I understand it's standard, but is it medically necessary right now for Camille specifically?"
- "Can we decline this and reassess later if needed?"

**If you feel rushed or pressured:**
- "We'd like a few minutes alone to discuss this."
- "Can you explain this in simpler terms?"
- "We need to understand why this is important before we agree."

### When to Push Back (Politely but Firmly)

⚠️ **Red flags** that may require advocacy:
- Staff talking *around* Camille instead of *to* her
- Interventions suggested without clear medical indication
- Pressure to speed up labor without medical necessity
- Dismissing Camille's pain, concerns, or preferences
- Changing plans without discussion or consent
- Creating unnecessary urgency or fear

**How to advocate**:
- "We appreciate your care, but we'd like to stick with our birth plan unless there's a medical reason to change."
- "Camille is doing great. Let's give her body more time before we intervene."
- "We understand your recommendation, but we're choosing to decline for now. We'll reassess if things change."
- "Please speak directly to Camille - she's the decision-maker here."

### When to Trust and Go With the Flow

✅ **Trust medical staff when**:
- There's a clear medical emergency (baby's heart rate dropping, heavy bleeding, etc.)
- They explain the "why" clearly and it makes sense
- Camille feels good about the recommendation
- Verenice agrees it's the right call

**Remember**: Medical staff are trained professionals. Most suggestions are genuinely in Camille's and baby's best interest. Advocacy doesn't mean fighting everything - it means asking questions, understanding the "why," and making informed choices together.

## 💪 Physical Support & Comfort Measures

### Labor Positions to Suggest

**Early Labor (at home)**:
- Walking, swaying, slow dancing
- Leaning forward on counters or furniture
- Hands and knees, cat-cow stretches
- Sitting on birth ball, gentle bouncing
- Side-lying rest between contractions

**Active Labor (hospital)**:
- Standing, leaning on David or bed
- Squatting (opens pelvis)
- Hands and knees (helps with back labor)
- Sitting backward on chair
- Supported lunges (one leg elevated)
- Birth ball sitting or leaning

**Transition (intense phase)**:
- Side-lying with peanut ball
- Semi-reclined with pillow support
- Hands and knees with head down
- Whatever position Camille requests (trust her body)

**Pushing Phase**:
- Side-lying (gentle, less tearing)
- Hands and knees
- Supported squatting
- Semi-reclined with leg support
- Standing/kneeling (gravity-assisted)

### Hands-On Comfort Techniques

**Counterpressure** (for back labor):
- Press firmly on lower back/sacrum during contractions
- Use palm, fist, or tennis ball in a sock
- Ask Camille where and how hard

**Massage**:
- Shoulders, neck, lower back (between contractions)
- Light touch on arms, legs (if she likes it)
- Scalp massage (can be very calming)

**Cold/Heat**:
- Cold washcloth on forehead, neck
- Heating pad or rice sock on lower back
- Ice chips for mouth

**Hip Squeezes**:
- Stand behind Camille (she's on hands and knees)
- Place hands on both hips, squeeze inward firmly during contractions

### What NOT to Do
- ❌ Don't touch her during contractions unless she asks
- ❌ Don't try to talk through contractions
- ❌ Don't suggest interventions (let medical staff or Verenice do that)
- ❌ Don't tell her to "calm down" or "relax" (this is hard work!)
- ❌ Don't take it personally if she snaps at you or changes her mind

## 🗣️ What to Say (and Not Say)

### 💚 Helpful Phrases

**During contractions**:
- "Breathe with me" (demonstrate slow breaths)
- "You're doing amazing"
- "Ride the wave, it's almost over"
- "I'm right here with you"
- "Let your body open"

- "That was a strong one - you handled it beautifully"

- "You are so strong"

**If she says "I can't do this"**:
- "You ARE doing this - you're incredible"

### 🚫 Phrases to Avoid

- ❌ "You're fine" (dismissive of her experience)
- ❌ "It could be worse" (not helpful)

- ❌ "Calm down" (implies she's overreacting)
- ❌ Any comparisons to other births
- ❌ Asking "Are you okay?" repeatedly (assume yes unless she says otherwise)

## 🕐 Labor Phases: What to Expect & How to Help

### Early Labor (Dilation 0-6cm)
**What's happening**: Contractions 5-20 min apart, manageable, cervix dilating slowly
**Duration**: Can last hours to days (especially first baby)
**Camille may feel**: Excited, nervous, chatty, able to rest

**Your job**:
- Keep things normal and calm at home as long as possible
- Time contractions but don't obsess
- Encourage rest, light snacks, hydration
- Distraction: watch TV, play games, walk around
- Protect her energy for active labor

**Don't rush to hospital** - stay home until 5-1-1 rule (unless water breaks or concerns)

---

### Active Labor (Dilation 6-8cm)
**What's happening**: Contractions 3-5 min apart, stronger, harder to talk through
**Duration**: 3-8 hours average
**Camille may feel**: Focused, serious, needs quiet, wants support

**Your job**:
- Stay close and attentive
- Help with position changes every 30-60 min
- Offer sips of water, ice chips
- Apply counterpressure if back labor
- Minimize talking during contractions
- Encourage her after each contraction
- Dim lights, play calming music (if she wants)

**Work with Verenice** - she'll guide comfort measures, you provide reassurance and physical support

---

### Transition (Dilation 8-10cm)
**What's happening**: Contractions 1-3 min apart, very intense, peak of labor
**Duration**: 15 min - 2 hours
**Camille may feel**: Overwhelmed, shaky, nauseous, doubt ("I can't do this"), irritable

**⚠️ This is the HARDEST phase - but it's also the shortest and means she's almost there**

**Your job**:
- Stay calm and grounded (even if she's panicking)
- Hold space - be a steady, quiet presence
- Remind her: "This is transition - you're almost done"
- Don't take it personally if she pushes you away or snaps
- Offer short, simple encouragement ("You've got this", "Almost there")
- Protect her space from unnecessary interruptions

**If she asks for epidural during transition**:
- Acknowledge her pain: "I know this is incredibly hard"
- Remind her how close she is: "You're in transition - almost time to push"
- Suggest waiting 10-15 more minutes to see if urge to push comes
- Support her decision either way - this is HER birth

---

### Pushing & Birth (Full dilation to baby)
**What's happening**: Urge to push, working with contractions to bring baby down
**Duration**: 30 min - 3 hours
**Camille may feel**: Relieved to push, empowered, exhausted, determined

**Your job**:
- Help her into pushing positions (hands and knees, side-lying, squatting)
- Hold her leg/back if needed
- Count through pushes if staff asks (or let them - follow her lead)
- Offer sips of water between contractions
- Tell her what you see: "I can see the baby's head!" "You're so close!"
- Be ready for the emotional moment when Cyrus arrives

**Don't**:
- Don't tell her how to push unless staff asks you to
- Don't look if you're squeamish about blood (stay near her head)
- Don't panic if there's blood or bodily fluids (normal!)

---

### Immediately After Birth
**What's happening**: Baby is born, skin-to-skin, placenta delivery, possible stitches
**Camille may feel**: Euphoric, exhausted, relieved, shaky, emotional

**Your job**:
- Celebrate this moment together!
- Take photos/videos if she's okay with it
- Help facilitate immediate skin-to-skin with Camille
- Ask to delay cord clamping if that's in birth plan (wait 1-3 min for blood transfer)
- You may be offered chance to cut cord
- Protect the first "golden hour" - minimal interruptions, skin-to-skin, bonding
- Thank the medical staff and Verenice

## 🏥 Hospital Environment Advocacy

### Protecting Camille's Birth Space

**Lighting**: Ask staff to dim lights or use lamps instead of overhead fluorescents
**Noise**: Request quiet voices, minimal chatter unrelated to labor
**Visitors**: Only people on the approved list - you are the gatekeeper
**Interruptions**: Politely ask staff to wait between contractions for non-urgent checks
**Modesty**: Make sure Camille is covered when she wants to be

### Managing Medical Staff

**Shift changes**: New nurses/doctors will introduce themselves - ask them to review birth plan
**Students/Observers**: You can decline to have students present if Camille prefers privacy
**Unnecessary checks**: Vaginal exams are not required on a schedule - she can decline unless there's a reason
**Continuous monitoring**: If low-risk, she may be able to use intermittent monitoring (allows more movement)

### Questions to Ask at Admission

- "Who is the attending OB on call today?"
- "Will we have the same nurse through labor, or will there be shift changes?"
- "Can we keep the lights dim and environment calm?"
- "Can Camille eat/drink during labor, or just ice chips?" (policies vary)
- "What's your policy on intermittent vs. continuous fetal monitoring?"

### Call for Verenice's Help When:
- You're not sure what Camille needs
- She's in back labor and counterpressure isn't working
- She's asking for pain relief and you're unsure how to respond
- Medical staff suggests intervention and you need a second opinion

### Call for Medical Staff When:
- Heavy bleeding (more than period-level)
- Baby's movements decrease significantly
- Camille has severe headache, vision changes, or chest pain
- Water breaks and fluid is brown/green (meconium)
- Camille feels strong urge to push (even if not "time" yet)
- Anything feels wrong or urgent

**Trust your gut** - if something feels off, speak up

### If There's a Medical Emergency

**Your role**:
- Stay calm (Camille will take cues from you)
- Get out of the way and let medical staff work
- Ask questions when there's a moment: "What's happening? What are you doing?"
- Hold Camille's hand, make eye contact, reassure her
- Trust the team - they train for emergencies

**Remember**: Most "emergencies" are precautionary and baby/mom end up fine. Try not to panic.

Labor can last 12-24+ hours. You need to stay strong for Camille.

### Self-Care Checklist
- [ ] Eat regular meals (even if small snacks)
- [ ] Stay hydrated (bring your own water bottle)
- [ ] Take bathroom breaks (ask Verenice or nurse to stay with Camille)
