# Habits

A framework for understanding how habits form, how to change them, and why most attempts at change fail. Based primarily on James Clear's *Atomic Habits*, which builds on Charles Duhigg's habit loop model and B.J. Fogg's behavioral design work.

> The core idea: **You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems.**

---

## The #1 Mistake: Changing the Wrong Thing

Most people try to change at the wrong level. There are three layers, and the order matters:

### The Three Layers of Behavior Change

```
┌─────────────────────────────┐
│       IDENTITY              │  ← Most effective (start here)
│    Beliefs, self-image,     │
│    worldview, judgments      │
├─────────────────────────────┤
│       PROCESS               │  ← Build systems here
│    Habits, routines,        │
│    systems, workflows       │
├─────────────────────────────┤
│       OUTCOMES              │  ← Least effective (don't start here)
│    Results, goals,          │
│    achievements             │
└─────────────────────────────┘
```

| Layer | Focus | Example |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Outcomes** (surface) | What you *get* | "I want to lose 20 lbs" |
| **Process** (middle) | What you *do* | "I go to the gym 3x per week" |
| **Identity** (core) | What you *believe* | "I am someone who moves their body daily" |

**The shift:** Instead of "I want to run a marathon" (outcome), ask "What would a runner do?" (identity). Every action is a vote for the type of person you want to become. You don't need a unanimous vote — just a majority.

## The Habit Loop

Every habit follows a four-step neurological pattern:

```
CUE → CRAVING → RESPONSE → REWARD
 ↑                              |
 └──────────────────────────────┘
        (feedback loop)
```

| Step | Role | Question It Answers |
|------|------|---------------------|
| **Cue** | Triggers the behavior | "What starts it?" |
| **Craving** | The motivation/desire | "What do I actually want?" |
| **Response** | The habit itself | "What do I do?" |
| **Reward** | The payoff that reinforces | "What do I get?" |

### Example: Afternoon Coffee

| Step | What Happens |
|------|-------------|
| Cue | It's 2pm and you feel tired |
| Craving | You want to feel alert and take a break |
| Response | You walk to the kitchen and make coffee |
| Reward | Energy boost + social interaction + mental break |

**Key insight:** The craving is never about the habit itself — it's about the *change in state* the habit delivers. You don't crave coffee. You crave alertness and a break.

---

## The Four Laws of Behavior Change

Each step of the habit loop maps to a law. Use these to **build** good habits or **break** bad ones:

### Building Good Habits

| Law | Habit Loop Step | Strategy |
|-----|----------------|----------|
| **Make it obvious** | Cue | Design your environment so the cue is visible |
| **Make it attractive** | Craving | Pair the habit with something you enjoy |
| **Make it easy** | Response | Reduce friction, use the 2-minute rule |
| **Make it satisfying** | Reward | Add immediate positive feedback |

### Breaking Bad Habits (invert each law)

| Law | Habit Loop Step | Strategy |
|-----|----------------|----------|
| **Make it invisible** | Cue | Remove triggers from your environment |
| **Make it unattractive** | Craving | Reframe the benefits, highlight the costs |
| **Make it difficult** | Response | Increase friction, add steps between you and the behavior |
| **Make it unsatisfying** | Reward | Add accountability, make consequences visible |

## Practical Techniques

### Habit Stacking
Attach a new habit to an existing one:
> "After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]."

Examples:
- "After I pour my morning coffee, I will write in my journal for 2 minutes"
- "After I sit down at my desk, I will write today's 3 priorities"
- "After I put my son to bed, I will lay out tomorrow's clothes"

### The Two-Minute Rule
When starting a new habit, scale it down to something that takes less than two minutes:

| Goal | Two-Minute Version |
|------|--------------------|
| Read before bed every night | Read one page |
| Run 3 miles | Put on running shoes |
| Study for class | Open your notes |
| Meditate for 20 minutes | Sit on the cushion for 2 minutes |

The point isn't the two minutes — it's becoming the type of person who *shows up*. You can optimize later. You can't optimize what doesn't exist.

### Environment Design
Your environment is the invisible hand that shapes behavior:
- **Want to eat better?** Put fruit on the counter, hide the snacks
- **Want to practice guitar?** Leave it on a stand in the living room, not in a case in the closet
- **Want to read more?** Put a book on your pillow
- **Want to reduce phone time?** Charge it in another room

> Every habit is initiated by a cue. You're more likely to notice cues that stand out.

### Temptation Bundling
Pair something you *need* to do with something you *want* to do:
- Listen to favorite podcasts only while exercising
- Watch a show you love only while folding laundry
- Get a coffee only after a morning walk

## Identity-Based Habits

The most powerful form of change starts with identity, not outcomes.

**The process:**
1. Decide the type of person you want to be
2. Prove it to yourself with small wins

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| "I want to quit smoking" | "I'm not a smoker" |
| "I want to lose weight" | "I'm the type of person who moves every day" |
| "I want to write a book" | "I'm a writer — I write every day" |
| "I want to be organized" | "I'm the type of person who maintains a clean space" |

Each time you perform the habit, you cast a vote for that identity. No single instance is transformative, but as the votes accumulate, the evidence builds and the identity becomes real.

## The Plateau of Latent Potential

Habits often feel like they aren't working because progress isn't linear. Results are delayed.

```
Expected:    /
            /
           /
          /
Actual:  ___________/
         (nothing)  (breakthrough)
```

This is the **Valley of Disappointment** — the gap between what you expect and what you get. Most people quit here because the effort doesn't seem to pay off. But the work *is* being stored. Like an ice cube that doesn't melt at 25, 26, 27, 28, 29, 30, 31 degrees — then suddenly melts at 32.

> Habits are the compound interest of self-improvement.

### Habit Tracking

Track weekly numbers to establish a baseline. Options:
- Simple checkbox in daily note
- Google Sheets for trend data over time
- Google Form for quick daily logging on mobile
- Obsidian's Keep The Rhythm plugin (already installed)

> The purpose of tracking isn't perfection — it's **awareness**. You can't improve what you don't measure.

## Quick Reference Card

**To build a habit:**
1. Make the cue obvious (environment design)
2. Make it attractive (temptation bundling)
3. Make it easy (two-minute rule)
4. Make it satisfying (track it, reward it)

**To break a habit:**
1. Make the cue invisible (remove triggers)
2. Make it unattractive (reframe the cost)
3. Make it hard (add friction)
4. Make it unsatisfying (add accountability)

**Remember:**
- Change your identity first, not your outcomes
- Systems > Goals
- 1% better every day = 37x better in a year
- You don't need motivation. You need environment design and systems.
