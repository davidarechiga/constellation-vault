# The complete Pokémon TCG expansion set encyclopedia

**From 102 cards in 1999 to over 250 per set today, the Pokémon Trading Card Game spans roughly 120 major English-language expansions across eight distinct eras.** Sealed booster boxes from the earliest sets now command $20,000–$60,000, while a single 1st Edition Base Set Charizard in perfect condition sold for $550,000 in late 2025. This guide walks through every major set chronologically, with current market values, top chase cards, and context for each — designed so someone with zero Pokémon knowledge can understand the full landscape. Prices reflect 2025–2026 market estimates and fluctuate; treat all figures as approximate midpoints.

---

## How to read this guide

A few terms you'll encounter throughout: A **booster box** is a sealed case of 36 randomized packs (the standard retail unit collectors invest in). **PSA 10** means a card graded "Gem Mint" by Professional Sports Authenticator — the highest grade, commanding the steepest premiums. A **chase card** is the single most valuable or desirable card to pull from a given set. **Secret rare** cards are numbered beyond the set's official count (e.g., card #107 in a 105-card set). **1st Edition** applies only to early sets (1999–2002) and denotes the first print run — dramatically more valuable than "Unlimited" reprints. Box prices listed are for **Unlimited English sealed boxes** unless noted. Special sets marked with ★ were not sold in traditional 36-pack booster boxes; prices reflect ETBs (Elite Trainer Boxes) or equivalent sealed products. All prices are in USD.

## Wizards of the Coast era (1999–2003): where it all began

The original 15 English expansions were published by Wizards of the Coast (the company behind Magic: The Gathering). These sets defined the hobby and remain the most valuable vintage Pokémon products. **Sealed boxes from this era range from $6,000 to $60,000**, and the era's top cards — Base Set Charizard, Neo Genesis Lugia, Crystal Charizard from Skyridge — are among the most expensive trading cards on Earth. Print quality issues (poor centering, inconsistent ink) mean PSA 10 copies are exceptionally rare for most WOTC holos, which amplifies their value.

**Base Set** launched everything — 102 cards including the legendary Charizard holo, the single most iconic trading card in history. A 1st Edition PSA 10 copy sold for **$550,000 at Heritage Auctions in December 2025**. The first three sets (Base, Jungle, Fossil) form the "holy trinity" of Kanto collecting. **Team Rocket** introduced Dark Pokémon and the game's first-ever secret rare (Dark Raichu). The **Neo series** (Genesis through Destiny) brought Generation II Pokémon and invented "Shining" cards — alternate-color predecessors to today's shinies. Neo Genesis Lugia in 1st Edition PSA 10 is one of the rarest cards in the hobby, with fewer than 50 copies graded that high. The era's final three sets — **Expedition, Aquapolis, and Skyridge** — featured e-Reader dot codes and the stunning Crystal-type secret rares. Skyridge, the very last WOTC set, had an extremely low print run and commands roughly **$50,000 per sealed box**, making it the most expensive WOTC product.

## EX era (2003–2007): the Gold Star golden age

When Nintendo/The Pokémon Company took over publishing from WOTC, they launched the EX series — 16 sets introducing Pokémon-ex (powerful cards that gave up two prizes when knocked out). The era's defining innovation was **Gold Star (★) cards**: shiny alternate-color Pokémon with pull rates around 1 per 2–3 boxes. These Gold Stars are now among the most expensive cards in the entire hobby, with Charizard Gold Star δ from Dragon Frontiers valued around **$60,000 in PSA 10**. Most EX-era sealed boxes are extremely scarce due to single print runs.

## Black & White era (2011–2013): Full Arts arrive

The BW era brought a complete visual overhaul — new card borders, a fresh art style, and the debut of **Full Art cards** (illustrations that extend across the entire card face). It also introduced Pokémon-EX (capital E-X, two-prize attackers) and secret rare "Gold" Trainer cards that remain collector favorites. Sealed box values range from **$2,500 to $7,000**, with several standout chase cards driving premiums.

## Sun & Moon era (2017–2020): GX cards and the alt art revolution

This era produced some of today's most valuable modern sealed products. **Team Up** booster boxes now sell for over $11,000, driven by the Latias & Latios GX alt art valued at roughly $10,000 in PSA 10. Hidden Fates, the era's standout special set, popularized the "Shiny Vault" concept that generated enormous consumer excitement.

## What 27 years of data tells us

Several clear patterns emerge across 120+ sets that any newcomer should understand.

**Charizard is the market's gravity well.** Across every era, Charizard cards consistently anchor the highest values — from Base Set ($500,000) through Flashfire ($3,200), Plasma Storm ($2,000), Burning Shadows ($1,000), Evolving Skies' Charizard VSTAR, to 151's SIR ($1,100). No other Pokémon comes close to this consistent market dominance.

**Eeveelutions are the second pillar.** Umbreon, Espeon, Sylveon, and the other Eeveelutions command outsized premiums in every era they appear. Evolving Skies and Prismatic Evolutions — both Eeveelution-focused sets — are the two most valuable modern boxes in their respective eras.

**Scarcity drives exponential value growth.** The relationship between print run and value is not linear. Skyridge ($50,000/box) and EX Team Rocket Returns ($45,000/box) had tiny print runs; Evolutions ($1,725/box) was printed into oblivion. A set's long-term value correlates more with sealed supply than with the popularity of its chase cards.

**Female Supporter Full Arts are a distinct asset class.** Starting in the Sun & Moon era, Full Art female Trainer/Supporter cards (Lillie, Lisia, Marnie, Cynthia, Iono) emerged as major price drivers, sometimes rivaling the Pokémon cards in value.

**The modern "alt art" era (2019–present) represents a paradigm shift.** Before Tag Team GX alternate arts, Pokémon cards were valued primarily for the Pokémon depicted and the card's rarity. Alt arts introduced narrative, painterly illustrations that collectors value as standalone art pieces — creating a new dimension of desirability that has driven the explosive growth of modern card values. The Moonbreon at $4,000 and the Latias & Latios at $10,000 are products of this shift.

For a newcomer looking to enter the hobby, the most approachable sealed products today are current-era booster boxes at $100–$285 and ETBs at $50–$130. Vintage sealed product is firmly in the investment/luxury category. The market rewards patience and knowledge — understanding which sets have low print runs, which chase cards resonate with the community, and where the next wave of nostalgia will hit.
